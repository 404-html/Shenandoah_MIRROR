The pace is slow and conversational, yet with a definite underlying sarcasm combined with hopelessness. Small elements work together to augment this complexity, such as the parentheses, or cliched phrase “beautiful new young wife.” line breaks and enjambment of the last line also emphasize the casual but also biting tone. I like the journey from the stillness of the here-and-now to the question in line four where the poem turns…I think that good poems usually don’t know the answer to the questions they ask, and while this question seems sarcastic or rhetorical, I think that the speaker genuinely wonders how the life and customs of men in Roman times haven’t changed. Presumably, Pliny the Younger’s ignorance to the terrifying beauty of the waterfall in his palace (and of his wife) resonates in the speaker’s life as well.

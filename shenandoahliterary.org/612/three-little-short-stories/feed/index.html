]]> By: sisoa15 https://shenandoahliterary.org/612/three-little-short-stories/#comment-264 Sat, 17 Mar 2012 17:19:42 +0000 http://shenandoahliterary.org/612/?p=54#comment-264 Achieving resonance with a story entitled “Three Little Love Stories” is a tremendously difficult task, yet Durban artlessly does so. I felt that her voice pushed forth the impact of this piece; it became personal, intimate, and raw. With each vignette, Durban conveys an evolution in experience and maturity that furthers the protagonist to an elevated understanding of love. In “Riddle Me This,” love’s passion is soiled with seeds of selfishness that sprout vengeance. This love is thus short-lived; the protagonist was not accepted for her true self, and was forced to uphold a marriage she did not fully believe in. In “All Hallow’s Eve,” lust is confused for love, and love is considered an avenue of escape from weighty emotional baggage. The protagonist and her lover share this core motive for seeking each other out, and thus attain an uneasy understanding that is better than the previous story’s love.
Lastly, in “Little Bone,” the protagonist reflects upon her long chain of experiences that have led her to cloud her view of life with pessimism. She fears a little bone because of all the negative potential it has, thus demonstrating the occupation of her mind with the consideration of fatalistic possibility. She finds comfort with her lover, who understands the way she is, and thus attempts to ease her. And, ultimately, through a mutual comprehension of one another, they are able to accept themselves and each other, and thus achieve a lasting, mature love.
Durban intimately communicates the values of the many degrees of love, and allows us to understand that the strongest love is based upon acceptance and understanding–dealing with issues rather than escaping from them, or ignoring them in favor of vengeance.

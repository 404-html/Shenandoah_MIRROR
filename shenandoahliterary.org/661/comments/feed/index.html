]]> Comment on BEYOND AUTOBIOGRAPHY: Claudia Emerson Through Three Poems on Race by denhamh20 https://shenandoahliterary.org/661/2016/12/11/beyond-autobiography-claudia-emerson-through-three-poems-on-race/#comment-400 Fri, 07 Apr 2017 19:22:40 +0000 http://shenandoahliterary.org/661/?p=249#comment-400 Wow. This is a masterful close reading of a selection of Claudia Emerson’s poems. I have never read work by her and I think that if I had, I would have missed out on its craft without Philip Belcher’s analysis and commentary. When I read “Elevator Operator,” I did not even pick up on the subject of the poem – the black woman who operates the elevator, who is a stark contrast to the white female shoppers that she transports. It reflects the whole point of her invisibility, which makes me wonder if I am that affected by my own white, privileged blindness, or if I was just manipulated by Emerson, to reflect that all along.

The broken, stair-step line structure of “Virginia Christian” is interesting because I’ve always had a problem truly deciphering it. But Belcher clears all that up by interpreting it as a a representation of the carried motion of the events, beyond the control of Christian because of who she is. Belcher’s reading of the characterization of the poem’s subject is refreshing: “She will quickly gather, however, that race in this poem cannot be viewed in isolation from gender.” Intersectionality of identity is something we study in my Women’s, Gender and Sexuality Studies and I love when I see academic and literary crossovers (which I think is a goal of Belcher’s piece here).

I definitely want to read more Emerson because of this and Belcher has started a seed in me for truly getting at her meaning.

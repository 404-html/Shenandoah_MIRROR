Comments for Volume 67, Number 1 https://shenandoahliterary.org/671 Just another Shenandoah site Sat, 24 Mar 2018 16:09:11 +0000 hourly 1 https://wordpress.org/?v=5.1.1 Comment on Mangoes by Jennifer Bagger https://shenandoahliterary.org/671/2017/09/10/mangoes-hashimoto/#comment-149 Sat, 24 Mar 2018 16:09:11 +0000 http://shenandoahliterary.org/671/?p=26#comment-149 “Mangoes” is delightfully bitter. There is nothing particularly laboring about any of the words alone, but together the poem exudes an itchy harshness through specific details such as the “Chevrolet’s tires [churning] with dust” and “the three black heads / of [her] family” as they disappear without looking back. The “shattered fruit” and the familiar and unfavorable feeling of “fruit flies [hovering] low” followed by the punchy declaration: “I don’t like Hawaii” similarly convey disturbance and the tense confusion of a child left behind. The use of the present tense effectively brings us into this scene of childhood, abandonment and stinging cuts in our mouths.

]]> Comment on Badlands by Jennifer Bagger https://shenandoahliterary.org/671/2017/09/07/badlands-lasorda/#comment-107 Sat, 03 Mar 2018 18:16:36 +0000 http://shenandoahliterary.org/671/?p=54#comment-107 This story is tightly tied together. I especially like the details that don’t at first glance have much to do with the progression of the story but in actuality provide information that better builds the world in which the story takes place. For example, the mention of the Mammoth burger that is served at the restaurant where Waylan works and the coinciding comment that it is “really too much meat for one serving” seems trivial but rather contributes to the barren wasteland that comes to represent the narrator’s relationship and life with Waylan. Additionally, the tactile imagery relating to the Badlands, the location of the narrator’s life with Waylan, evokes emptiness and death. The phantom pregnancy, a non-physical manifestation of a loveless relationship, loses significance over time as it never truly existed.
